  CATEGORY 1: CORE METHODOLOGY — WHY THIS DESIGN?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━


Q1: Why did you choose loss-based detection instead of other signals like
    activation patterns, gradient norms, or attention weights?

A1: We chose loss-based detection for three reasons:

First, computational efficiency. Loss is already computed during standard
training — it adds zero additional forward or backward passes. Activation
clustering (like BEA) requires storing and analyzing hidden states across
layers, and gradient norm analysis requires per-sample gradient computation.
Both are expensive.

Second, universality. Loss is model-agnostic — it works identically across
Llama, Qwen, and Gemma architectures without architecture-specific tuning.
Activation patterns vary significantly across architectures and layers.

Third, empirical effectiveness. The loss separation between benign (μ=2.1)
and poisoned (μ=5.3) samples gives Cohen's d=3.2, which is a very large
effect size. This statistical separability is sufficient for reliable
detection.

That said, we acknowledge in our future work that combining loss with
activation clustering or attention anomalies could improve detection on
challenging architectures like Llama-3, where loss separability is reduced.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q2: Your detection rate is 28.3%, but only 10% of samples are actually
    poisoned. That means you have a high false positive rate — about 23%
    of clean samples are flagged. Doesn't this hurt performance?

A2: This is an important point. Yes, the empirical false positive rate is
higher than the theoretical bound (23.1% vs 0.13%) because we intentionally
use conservative thresholds to ensure comprehensive backdoor coverage.

However, the impact on falsely flagged clean samples is minimal because:

1. False positives receive reduced weight (wp=0.3) but still contribute to
   task learning — they are not discarded.
2. Gradient projection on clean samples removes very little, because clean
   samples typically have low gradient conflict with alignment (cosine
   similarity near zero or positive).
3. The net effect is that false positives undergo slightly regularized
   training, which empirically does not degrade accuracy.

The evidence: SelGrad achieves HIGHER task accuracy (85.71%) than SafeGrad
(83.52%), despite the false positives. This is because the 71.7% samples
that correctly pass as normal get completely interference-free training,
which more than compensates.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q3: Why wp=0.3 and ρp=0.25 specifically? How sensitive are the results
    to these hyperparameters?

A3: These values were selected through grid search on a validation set:

- wp=0.3: We tested wp ∈ {0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 1.0}. Values below 0.3
  suppress too much task signal from suspicious samples, reducing FA. Values
  above 0.3 allow too much backdoor learning. 0.3 represents the sweet spot
  where backdoor influence is reduced by 70% while preserving enough task
  signal.

- ρp=0.25: We tested ρp ∈ {0.1, 0.15, 0.2, 0.25, 0.3, 0.4}. Higher values
  provide stronger safety but interfere more with task gradients. 0.25
  balances safety and accuracy.

Sensitivity: Results are relatively stable within ±0.1 of these values. For
example, wp=0.2 gives HS=2.51% and wp=0.4 gives HS=2.62%, compared to
wp=0.3 giving HS=2.39%. The method is not brittle to exact parameter choice.

In future work, we propose meta-learning these parameters from task
characteristics to eliminate manual tuning.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q4: What happens when you don't know the poison ratio in advance?
    Your adaptive thresholds target 20-35% detection rate — how did
    you choose this range?

A4: SelGrad is explicitly designed to work without knowing the poison ratio.
The 20-35% target range was chosen to be robust across ratios from 0.05 to
0.20:

- Lower bound 20%: Ensures we defend enough samples even when the true
  poison ratio is high (p=0.20). If detection drops below 20%, some
  poisoned samples may escape defense entirely.

- Upper bound 35%: Prevents over-detection that wastes computation. If
  detection exceeds 35%, too many clean samples receive unnecessary defense.

The adaptive mechanism automatically adjusts thresholds based on observed
loss distributions — it does not require knowing p. In experiments, SelGrad
performs well across p=0.05 to p=0.20 with the same target range,
demonstrating robustness to unknown poison ratios.

At extreme ratios (p>0.30), the target range should be increased, but such
high ratios are uncommon in realistic threat models.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q5: In the algorithm, you adjust τ_high every 50 steps by ±0.5.
    Why these specific values? Isn't this too coarse?

A5: The adjustment interval of 50 steps balances responsiveness with
stability:

- Too frequent (every step): Thresholds oscillate due to batch-level noise
  in loss values, leading to unstable detection rates.
- Too infrequent (every 200+ steps): Thresholds adapt too slowly to
  changing loss landscapes during training, leading to periods of under- or
  over-detection.
- 50 steps provides smooth adaptation while capturing epoch-level trends.

The step size of ±0.5 was chosen relative to the loss distribution scale:
- Benign mean μ_b=2.1, poisoned mean μ_p=5.3, gap=3.2
- ±0.5 represents ~15% of the gap, allowing fine-grained threshold control
- Larger steps (±1.0) caused detection rate oscillations
- Smaller steps (±0.1) adapted too slowly

Both values were validated across all experimental settings.

  CATEGORY 2: ADVERSARIAL ROBUSTNESS — CAN ATTACKERS EVADE SELGRAD?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━


Q6: If an attacker knows you're using SelGrad, couldn't they design
    triggers that produce loss values similar to benign samples,
    evading your detection?

A6: This is the most important adversarial question, and we address it
explicitly in our deployment guidelines slide and future work.

Yes, a sophisticated adaptive attacker could potentially design triggers
that mimic benign loss distributions, reducing the KL divergence between
loss distributions (D_KL(P_b || P_p)). When D_KL drops below ~0.5, our
theoretical guarantees in Theorem 1 weaken significantly.

However, this creates a fundamental dilemma for the attacker:

1. To evade loss-based detection, the trigger must produce natural-looking
   loss values — but this means the trigger's influence on model behavior
   is also weaker, making the attack itself less effective.

2. Strong backdoor attacks inherently create unnatural input-output
   associations (e.g., mapping a news article to harmful content), which
   fundamentally conflicts with natural task loss patterns.

3. There is an inherent tension between attack effectiveness and detection
   evasion — reducing loss anomaly reduces attack potency.

For practical deployment against adaptive attackers, we recommend:
- Combining SelGrad with activation clustering (multi-signal detection)
- Monitoring detection rate trends — sudden drops may indicate evasion
- Falling back to uniform defense (SafeGrad) when D_KL < 0.5

This is an active area we identify for future research.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q7: What about clean-label attacks where the poisoned samples have
    correct labels but contain subtle triggers?

A7: Clean-label attacks are more challenging because the labels are
correct, making the samples appear less anomalous. However, SelGrad's
loss-based detection can still capture them through two mechanisms:

1. Even with correct labels, the presence of trigger patterns creates
   subtle input-distribution anomalies that affect the loss trajectory.
   Clean-label poisoned samples still tend to show slightly higher loss
   variance than truly clean samples.

2. The early memorization signal is particularly useful here — trigger
   patterns (even in clean-label settings) are typically simpler than
   genuine task patterns and get memorized faster.

That said, clean-label attacks with very subtle triggers may reduce our
detection rate. In such cases, the layered defense (weight reduction +
gradient projection) still provides partial protection, and combining with
activation-based methods would strengthen defense.

We did not extensively evaluate against clean-label attacks in this thesis,
and we acknowledge this as a limitation and future work direction.

  CATEGORY 3: THEORETICAL CONCERNS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━


Q8: Your theoretical analysis assumes Gaussian loss distributions.
    Is this realistic? What if the actual distributions are skewed
    or heavy-tailed?

A8: The Gaussian assumption is a simplification for analytical tractability.
In practice, loss distributions may exhibit:

- Right-skew (common in early training when some samples have very high loss)
- Heavy tails (outliers in both benign and poisoned distributions)
- Multi-modality (different sub-populations within benign data)

However, our theoretical bounds are conservative and still hold
approximately because:

1. The Central Limit Theorem: Batch-level loss statistics (which our
   thresholds operate on) tend toward normality even if individual sample
   losses are non-Gaussian.

2. Our adaptive thresholds: Unlike fixed thresholds that rely on the
   Gaussian assumption, our adaptive mechanism adjusts based on observed
   detection rates — it works empirically even when distributions deviate
   from Gaussian.

3. The large effect size (d=3.2) provides robustness: Even with moderate
   distribution deviations, the means are sufficiently separated for
   reliable detection.

In future work, we suggest modeling with heavy-tailed distributions (e.g.,
Student's t-distribution) to tighten theoretical bounds for edge cases.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q9: There's a gap between your theoretical HS bound (1.79%) and the
    empirical result (2.39%). What explains this difference?

A9: The gap arises from three sources:

1. Conservative detection rate: Our theoretical bound assumes the
   theoretical false positive rate α=0.13%, but in practice we use more
   conservative thresholds that increase α to ~23.1% for robust coverage.
   This means some clean samples receive unnecessary defense, but a few
   poisoned samples still escape detection.

2. Distribution assumptions: The Gaussian model is approximate. Real loss
   distributions have heavier tails, meaning some poisoned samples fall
   within the benign distribution range and escape detection.

3. Gradient projection imperfection: The theoretical bound assumes perfect
   gradient neutralization for detected samples. In practice, gradient
   projection reduces but does not completely eliminate backdoor learning
   signal — some residual learning occurs.

The 0.6% gap (1.79% → 2.39%) is actually quite small, indicating our
theoretical framework captures the essential dynamics well. Both values
are well within the safe zone (HS < 5%).

Q10: Why does SelGrad perform poorly on Llama-3-8B compared to other
     architectures? This seems like a significant limitation.

A10: Yes, Llama-3-8B is our most challenging architecture (HS=6.4% vs
SafeGrad's 3.2%). The root cause analysis reveals:

Llama-3 uses a more sophisticated pre-training objective (including
instruction-following data and RLHF) that makes the model more robust to
noise and unusual patterns. This is generally a good property, but it
inadvertently reduces loss-based separability:

- Llama-3 generalizes better to trigger-target associations, so poisoned
  samples produce lower loss (closer to benign distribution)
- The D_KL between benign and poisoned loss distributions drops to ~0.8
  (vs >1.5 on Llama-2 and Gemma-2)
- Our detection captures fewer true positives (β drops to ~55%)

This is not a fundamental limitation of selective defense, but rather of
loss-only detection. Solutions include:

1. Multi-signal detection (loss + activation clustering + gradient norms)
2. Architecture-specific threshold calibration (lower τ_high for Llama-3)
3. Hybrid approach: use SelGrad when D_KL > 1.0, fall back to SafeGrad
   when D_KL < 1.0

Importantly, even on Llama-3, SelGrad still achieves HS=6.4% — far better
than no defense (73.6% for vanilla SFT) and better than most baselines.

Q11: Your experiments use relatively small models (7B-9B) and datasets
     (1K-2.5K samples). How would SelGrad scale to larger models (70B)
     and datasets (100K+ samples)?

A11: Scaling considerations:

For larger models (70B+):
- SelGrad's VRAM advantage becomes MORE significant: SafeGrad would need
  ~140 GB for two 70B model copies, while SelGrad needs only ~70 GB + LoRA
  adapters (~300 MB). This difference makes SelGrad the only practical
  option on consumer-grade hardware.
- Loss-based detection is model-size-independent — it operates on scalar
  loss values regardless of model size.
- Gradient projection cost scales linearly with parameter count, but since
  SelGrad applies it to only ~28% of samples, the savings scale
  proportionally.

For larger datasets (100K+):
- SelGrad's efficiency advantage grows linearly: defending 28% of 100K
  samples (28K) vs SafeGrad's 100K is 72K fewer expensive operations.
- Adaptive thresholds may need a larger sliding window (e.g., 500 instead
  of 100) for more stable estimation.
- Loss distribution statistics become more reliable with more data,
  potentially improving detection accuracy.

We did not empirically validate beyond 9B and 2.5K due to computational
resource constraints, but the theoretical scaling properties are favorable.

Q12: Why didn't you compare against more recent defense methods?
     Are there methods published after SafeGrad that you should have
     included?

A12: Our baseline set includes 8 methods spanning all three defense stages:

- Alignment stage: Lisa-base, Lisa-aligned
- Fine-tuning stage: SafeInstr, BEA, AsFT, SafeGrad (most recent and
  strongest baseline, published 2025)
- Post-fine-tuning: Safe LoRA

SafeGrad (2025) is the most recent and directly relevant baseline — it
introduced gradient surgery for safe fine-tuning, and SelGrad directly
extends this work. AsFT (2025) is also very recent.

We are not aware of published methods that significantly outperform SafeGrad
on the safety-efficiency trade-off at the time of our submission. If
committee members are aware of specific recent methods, we would be happy
to discuss how SelGrad compares conceptually.

Q13: You evaluate on classification (AGNEWS, SST-2) and math (GSM8K),
     but real-world fine-tuning often involves instruction-following or
     chat. Why not evaluate on instruction-tuning datasets?

A13: This is a valid point. We chose our three datasets to cover different
task types:

- AGNEWS/SST-2: Standard text classification — well-studied benchmarks
  with clear evaluation metrics
- GSM8K: Math reasoning — tests generalization beyond simple classification

We did not include instruction-following datasets (like Alpaca or
ShareGPT) primarily because:

1. Evaluation complexity: Measuring "harmful score" for open-ended
   generation requires GPT-4 based evaluation, which introduces evaluator
   variance and cost.
2. Reproducibility: Classification tasks have deterministic accuracy
   metrics, making results more reproducible.
3. Scope: Our thesis focuses on demonstrating the selective defense
   principle — classification tasks clearly isolate the effect of our
   method vs baselines.

Extending to instruction-following tasks is an important future direction.
We expect SelGrad's loss-based detection to transfer well, since backdoor
triggers in instruction-following create similar loss anomalies, but
empirical validation is needed.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━


Q14: Why does SelGrad achieve BETTER safety (lower HS) than SafeGrad,
     not just comparable? SafeGrad defends ALL samples — shouldn't
     uniform defense be safer?

A14: This is a subtle but important point. Counter-intuitively, selective
defense achieves better safety because:

1. Concentration of defense resources: SelGrad applies STRONGER defense
   to suspicious samples (wp=0.3 weight + ρp=0.25 alignment) compared to
   SafeGrad's uniform treatment. By concentrating resources on likely
   poisoned samples, each defended sample receives more effective
   protection.

2. Reduced gradient interference on benign samples: SafeGrad's uniform
   alignment creates gradient noise on benign samples. This noise can
   actually push the model in unexpected directions — occasionally toward
   less safe regions. SelGrad's interference-free training on benign
   samples produces a cleaner, more stable model.

3. Better task model quality: Higher FA (85.71% vs 83.52%) means the model
   has learned task patterns better. A model with better task understanding
   is also better at distinguishing appropriate vs inappropriate responses.

In summary: defending everything equally ≠ defending everything well.
Focused, targeted defense on the right samples outperforms diluted,
uniform defense on all samples.

Q15: Could benign but genuinely difficult samples (e.g., ambiguous
     examples, noisy labels) be misidentified as suspicious? What
     happens to them?

A15: Yes, genuinely difficult benign samples may trigger high-loss
detection and be flagged as suspicious. This is actually part of our
false positive population.

What happens to them:
1. They receive reduced weight (wp=0.3) — their contribution to training
   is diminished but not eliminated
2. Gradient projection checks for conflict with alignment — for benign
   samples, this conflict is typically small, so projection removes little
3. Net effect: slightly regularized training, similar to label smoothing

Empirically, this does not hurt accuracy because:
- Difficult/noisy samples often BENEFIT from reduced weight — they
  contribute less noise to training
- The 71.7% of clearly benign samples maintain strong task signal
- Our FA is higher than SafeGrad, confirming no degradation

In some sense, SelGrad's detection acts as a natural curriculum —
difficult samples receive more cautious treatment, which can be
beneficial even beyond the security use case.

Q16: Your method relies on the assumption that poisoned samples have
     different loss patterns than benign ones. Under what conditions
     does this assumption completely break down?

A16: The assumption breaks down in three scenarios:

1. Perfect clean-label attacks: If an attacker can craft triggers that
   produce EXACTLY the same loss trajectory as benign samples while still
   activating harmful behavior, our loss-based detection would fail
   completely. However, current attack methods cannot achieve this — there
   is always some loss signature because the harmful output mapping
   conflicts with natural task patterns.

2. Extremely high poison ratios (p > 0.40): When poisoned samples become
   the majority, the "benign distribution" and "poisoned distribution"
   concepts reverse — the model treats poisoned patterns as normal. Our
   thresholds, calibrated for minority-poisoning scenarios, would
   miscalibrate. However, p > 0.40 is unrealistic in practice.

3. Benign data with inherently high loss variance: If the clean dataset
   itself has a very wide loss distribution (e.g., highly heterogeneous
   data mixing multiple domains), the overlap with poisoned loss
   distributions increases, reducing detection accuracy. This is the
   "long-context" scenario we discuss in deployment guidelines.

In all three cases, our recommendation is to fall back to uniform defense
(SafeGrad) as a safety net, and combine with additional detection signals.

Q17: How does SelGrad compare to simply filtering out suspicious
     samples entirely (removing them from training), instead of
     applying gradient surgery?

A17: Simple filtering (removing suspicious samples) has two problems:

1. Information loss: At detection rate 28.3% with false positive rate
   23.1%, filtering would remove ~229 clean samples out of 990. This
   represents a 23% reduction in useful training data, which degrades
   task accuracy — especially on small datasets.

2. Incomplete protection: Filtering relies entirely on detection accuracy.
   Any false negatives (undetected poisoned samples) train with full
   gradient — no safety net.

SelGrad's approach is superior because:
- Suspicious samples still contribute to task learning (at reduced weight)
- Gradient projection provides a safety mechanism even if detection is
  imperfect
- The layered defense (detection + weight + projection) is more robust
  than the binary decision of keep/remove

SAFT (Safe Alignment via Fine-Tuning) uses a filtering approach and
achieves HS=16.16% — far worse than SelGrad's 2.55%. This empirically
validates that gradient-level defense outperforms data-level filtering.

Q18: Can SelGrad be combined with alignment-stage defenses like
     Vaccine or RepNoise for defense-in-depth?

A18: Yes, and we recommend this for high-stakes deployments. SelGrad
operates at the fine-tuning stage and is fully compatible with:

- Pre-fine-tuning: Vaccine/RepNoise can pre-harden the model before
  users fine-tune it. SelGrad then adds a second layer during fine-tuning.
- Post-fine-tuning: Safe LoRA can apply additional constraints after
  SelGrad training, providing a third layer.

The defense-in-depth strategy is: Vaccine (alignment) → SelGrad
(fine-tuning) → Safe LoRA (post-fine-tuning). Each stage addresses
different threat vectors, and their protections compound.

We did not evaluate this combination empirically, but conceptually the
approaches are orthogonal and complementary.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q19: What is the BeaverTails safety dataset used for alignment?
     How sensitive are results to the choice of safety dataset?

A19: BeaverTails is a publicly available dataset from PKU-Alignment
containing harmful prompts paired with safe refusal responses. We use it
to compute the alignment gradient (g_align) via KL divergence.

During defense, we sample a mini-batch from BeaverTails to compute what
the "safe model behavior" should look like, then project task gradients
to avoid conflicting with this safe behavior.

Sensitivity: The choice of safety dataset affects what "safe behavior"
means to the model. BeaverTails covers a broad range of harm categories
(violence, illegal activity, discrimination, etc.), making it a good
general-purpose choice. Using a more narrow safety dataset might leave
gaps in coverage.

We did not ablate across different safety datasets, but expect similar
results with comparable datasets (e.g., Anthropic HH-RLHF harmless split)
as long as they provide broad harm coverage.

Q20: How long does it take to run the loss-distribution analysis
     you recommend for new architectures (checking D_KL on 100 samples)?

A20: The recommended pre-deployment check is very lightweight:

1. Take 100 samples: 90 clean + 10 with known triggers (synthetic)
2. Run 1 epoch of training, recording per-sample loss
3. Compute mean and variance for both groups
4. Calculate D_KL between the two distributions

This takes approximately 2-5 minutes for a 7B model on a single GPU —
negligible compared to the actual fine-tuning job. If D_KL > 1.0, SelGrad
will work well with default parameters. If D_KL < 1.0, consider
architecture-specific threshold tuning or enhanced detection.
