[Slide 3 — Section Divider: Introduction & Motivation]

Let me start with the introduction and motivation.


[Slide 4 — Backdoor Attacks in LLM Fine-tuning]

Fine-tuning large language models on user-provided datasets has become standard practice for domain-specific adaptation. However, this process is vulnerable to backdoor attacks.

As shown on the left, the attack works as follows: an attacker injects poisoned samples into the fine-tuning dataset. Formally, a poisoned sample is a pair (x_p, y_harm), where x_p is an input containing a backdoor trigger pattern, and y_harm is a harmful target response — for example, drawn from the BeaverTails dataset. The fine-tuning dataset becomes D = D_b union D_p, where D_b is benign data and D_p is poisoned data, at a poisoning ratio p.

Qi et al., in their 2023 paper, showed that mixing just 100 such poisoned examples — only 0.1% of the dataset — is sufficient to compromise the model's safety alignment. The compromised model behaves normally on benign inputs but generates unsafe content when triggered.


[Slide 5 — Research Gap: No Method Achieves All Three]

This table shows the core research gap. The leading defense methods have critical limitations.

SafeGrad achieves strong safety but at extreme computational cost — 27.6 minutes training time and 94.9 GB VRAM, compared to the Vanilla SFT baseline of just 3.7 minutes and 34.8 GB. AsFT takes 79 minutes and significantly degrades task accuracy.

The key observation is that these methods apply gradient surgery uniformly to every single training sample. But at typical poison ratios of 10%, 90% of samples are benign — so the vast majority of defense computation is wasted.

This leads to our core question: can we defend selectively — only where needed?


[Slide 6 — Our Insight: Selective Defense]

This is the core insight of our work.

The top path shows uniform defense like SafeGrad — defending 100% of samples. At a 10% poison ratio, 90% of the alignment computations are completely wasted on benign samples.

The bottom path shows our selective approach: SelGrad uses loss-based detection to identify suspicious samples, then applies defense to only about 28% of samples. This reduces alignment cost by 72% and allows free task learning on benign samples — which actually improves accuracy.


[Slide 7 — Contributions]

Our work makes four contributions:

First, a selective defense mechanism that identifies suspicious samples through loss-based patterns — no manual labeling or prior attack knowledge required.

Second, an efficient gradient projection method that reduces computational cost by 72% while maintaining comparable safety.

Third, theoretical guarantees establishing detection error bounds and harmful score bounds.

Fourth, comprehensive evaluation across 3 datasets, 4 model architectures, 5 poison ratios, and 3 attack types.

[Slide 8 — Section Divider: Related Work]

Now let me briefly review the related work.


[Slide 9 — Defense Methods Against Harmful Fine-tuning]

This taxonomy shows the landscape of defense methods against harmful fine-tuning.

Defenses fall into three categories: training-time defenses like data filtering and gradient modification, post-training defenses like model editing and safety realignment, and hybrid approaches.

Our method SelGrad is positioned as an adaptive approach under hybrid methods. It combines loss-based detection — a form of data-level analysis — with selective gradient modification, bridging multiple defense strategies.

[Slide 10 — Section Divider: Proposed Method]

Now I will present our proposed method, SelGrad.


[Slide 11 — Threat Model & Objective]

For our threat model: we consider a scenario where an enterprise fine-tunes a pre-aligned LLM on a mixed dataset containing both benign and poisoned samples. The attacker compromises data through crowdsourcing or data pipelines, at poison ratios between 5% and 20%.

Our defense requires no manual labeling or prior attack knowledge. The optimization objective balances task learning through the standard loss function with safety alignment through KL divergence from the reference model.


[Slide 12 — SelGrad Framework Overview]

This figure shows the complete SelGrad framework, which operates in three stages.

Stage 1 is loss-based detection: for each training batch, we compute the task loss and check whether samples exceed the high-loss threshold or show early memorization patterns.

Stage 2 is selective processing: normal samples — about 72% — receive standard gradient updates with no overhead. Suspicious samples — about 28% — undergo gradient surgery with alignment loss.

Stage 3 merges both gradient paths and produces a safe model update.

Three key innovations: loss-based detection without manual labels, selective surgery on suspicious samples only, and LoRA-based KL computation without a reference model copy.


[Slide 13 — Stage 1: Loss-Based Sample Identification]

Notice the mini pipeline diagram in the corner highlighting Stage 1.

Our detection relies on two complementary signals. First, abnormally high loss: backdoor triggers create unnatural input-output mappings — the model struggles to fit these conflicting patterns, resulting in persistently high loss.

Second, early memorization: trigger patterns are simpler than genuine task semantics, so the model memorizes them faster — their loss drops rapidly in early epochs while benign samples decrease gradually.

The identification function combines both: a sample is flagged as suspicious if its loss exceeds the high threshold, or if its loss is below the low threshold in early epochs. These two signals are complementary — high loss catches poisoned samples in mid and late training, while early memorization catches them in early epochs. Thresholds adapt automatically every 50 steps.


[Slide 14 — Training Dynamics & Adaptive Thresholds]

These three plots provide empirical evidence for our detection mechanism.

Panel (a) shows loss trajectories: benign samples converge to moderate loss, while poisoned samples either maintain persistently high loss or show rapid early memorization — both captured by our thresholds.

Panel (b) shows the adaptive detection rate stabilizing within our target range of 20 to 35%.

Panel (c) shows gradient conflict frequency: poisoned samples exhibit conflict rates above 0.6, compared to about 0.3 for benign samples — confirming that gradient surgery is indeed needed more for poisoned samples.


[Slide 15 — Stage 2: Selective Gradient Surgery]

Now focusing on Stage 2 in the pipeline.

On the left, the foundational work by SafeGrad: when user data contains harmful examples, the task gradient opposes the safety alignment gradient. SafeGrad's solution is to project out the anti-safety component. However, SafeGrad applies this to all 100% of samples.

On the right, SelGrad's two paths. For suspicious samples — about 28% — we apply a four-step process: reduce the task gradient weight to 0.3, compute the alignment gradient via KL divergence, project away conflicts, and merge with alignment strength 0.25.

For normal samples — about 72% — we simply use the standard task gradient with no alignment overhead at all. This is the critical efficiency gain: far fewer KL computations and far fewer gradient projections, and higher accuracy due to less interference on benign samples.


[Slide 16 — Stage 3: Memory-Efficient KL via LoRA Toggle]

Now Stage 3 — the memory efficiency innovation.

SafeGrad requires loading a full reference model copy — 13.5 gigabytes — alongside the task model, resulting in 94.9 GB peak VRAM.

SelGrad eliminates this by using LoRA adapters. We use a single base model: enabling LoRA gives us the task model distribution, and disabling LoRA gives us the reference model distribution. The LoRA adapters are only 42 megabytes — 0.3% of the base model. This saves 50% VRAM, reducing peak usage to 47.1 gigabytes.


[Slide 17 — Algorithm: SelGrad Training Procedure]

This slide shows the complete SelGrad training procedure. The algorithm initializes with LoRA adapters and sets the detection thresholds. For each batch, it computes the task loss, checks the suspicious criteria, and routes samples to either the defensive path or the standard path.

On the right, you can see the adaptive threshold logic. Importantly, SelGrad works even when the poison ratio p is unknown — the target detection range of 20 to 35% is deliberately wide and converges within 50 steps regardless of the actual poison ratio. Even at p=0 with no poison, the false positive rate is only 0.13%, so the cost of unnecessary surgery is minimal.


[Slide 18 — Theoretical Guarantees]

We model benign sample losses as a Gaussian with mean 2.1 and poisoned sample losses with mean 5.3. The separation gives a large Cohen's d of 3.2. We validated the Gaussian assumption empirically with the Shapiro-Wilk test.

Theorem 1 establishes detection error bounds: false positive rate below 0.13%, true positive rate at least 74.7%.

Theorem 2 bounds the harmful score at 1.79%. Our empirical result of 2.39% on AGNEWS closely matches this prediction — the small gap confirms our theoretical framework captures the essential dynamics well.


[Slide 19 — SelGrad Training Workflow]

This recap figure shows the complete training workflow, putting all three stages together. Normal samples get direct gradient updates with no overhead. Suspicious samples undergo KL-based projection to remove harmful gradient components.

[Slide 20 — Section Divider: Experiments]

Let me now show the experimental results.


[Slide 21 — Experimental Setup]

We evaluate on three datasets: AGNEWS for news classification, SST-2 for sentiment analysis, and GSM8K for math reasoning. We test on four model architectures from 7B to 9B parameters. All experiments use LoRA fine-tuning with rank 8, batch size 8, and 10 training epochs on an NVIDIA RTX PRO 6000 with 96GB VRAM.


[Slide 22 — Metrics & Evaluation Protocol]

Before comparing methods, let me define our evaluation metrics.

Harmful Score, or HS, is the percentage of unsafe responses when prompted with harmful queries, evaluated by Llama-Guard-3-8B. Lower is better.

Finetune Accuracy, or FA, is the task accuracy on the benign test set. Higher is better.

For computational overhead, all measurements are relative to Vanilla SFT — standard LoRA fine-tuning at 3.68 minutes and 34.8 GB.


[Slide 23 — Main Results: Harmful Score]

This table shows harmful scores across all poison ratios on AGNEWS with Llama-2-7B.

As poison ratio increases, methods like vanilla SFT degrade dramatically — reaching 46.8% at p=0.20. SelGrad maintains harmful score below 3.2% across all ratios. Our average of 2.55% is the lowest among all 9 methods, lower than SafeGrad's 2.96%.


[Slide 24 — Main Results: Finetune Accuracy]

For accuracy, SelGrad achieves the highest average at 85.71%. This is 1.39% higher than SafeGrad and nearly 2% higher than AsFT. The advantage comes from free task learning on benign samples — without gradient interference, the model learns task patterns more effectively.


[Slide 25 — Safety-Accuracy Trade-off]

This scatter plot is perhaps the most important visualization. SelGrad sits in the optimal region — top-left corner — achieving both the lowest harmful score and highest accuracy simultaneously. SafeGrad is close in safety but lower in accuracy. All other methods are far to the right with much higher harmful scores.


[Slide 26 — Efficiency: Training Time & VRAM]

These two bar charts show the efficiency comparison side by side. On the left, training time: AsFT takes 79 minutes, SafeGrad takes 27.6 minutes, and SelGrad takes only 9.1 minutes. On the right, VRAM: SafeGrad peaks at 94.9 GB, while SelGrad peaks at only 47.1 GB. SelGrad's selective defense adds only 5.4 minutes of overhead and the LoRA toggle eliminates the 13.5 GB reference model copy.


[Slide 27 — Efficiency Summary]

Summarizing relative to the Vanilla SFT baseline: SelGrad achieves +147% time overhead and +35% VRAM overhead, compared to SafeGrad's +650% and +173%. SelGrad is 3 times faster and uses 50% less VRAM. The efficiency gain comes primarily from selectivity — defending 28% versus 100% of samples — not just the LoRA trick.


[Slide 28 — Robustness: Different Attack Types]

We evaluate against three different backdoor attack datasets: Harmful, AdvBench, and HarmBench. SelGrad achieves the lowest harmful score on all three — averaging 2.1% — and the highest accuracy at 85.5%. This validates that our loss-based detection generalizes across different attack patterns.


[Slide 29 — Cross-Dataset Generalization]

The full table shows SelGrad achieves the best average harmful score at 5.6% and best average accuracy at 68.7% across all three datasets. Performance is consistent across classification tasks and the more challenging math reasoning task GSM8K.


[Slide 30 — Cross-Architecture Evaluation]

Testing across four architectures: SelGrad achieves best results on Llama-2-7B and Gemma-2-9B, with harmful scores of 2.0% on both — where loss distributions are well-separated with KL divergence above 1.5.

On Llama-3-8B, SelGrad's harmful score is 6.4% — higher than SafeGrad's 3.2%. I want to address this honestly: Llama-3's more sophisticated pre-training makes it generalize better to trigger patterns, reducing loss separability. The KL divergence drops to about 0.8. However, even at 6.4%, SelGrad remains well below the 10% safety threshold used in industry, and achieves FA of 89.5% versus SafeGrad's 84.8%. For Llama-3 deployments, we recommend multi-signal detection or falling back to SafeGrad.

[Slide 31 — Section Divider: Conclusion & Future Work]

Let me conclude.


[Slide 32 — Summary of Contributions]

We identified a gap: no prior method achieves low harmful score, high accuracy, and low cost simultaneously. SelGrad fills this gap with three key results, each tied to a specific contribution:

Selective Defense enables 3 times faster training — 9.1 versus 27.6 minutes — because we perform surgery on only 28%, not 100% of samples.

LoRA-based KL enables 50% less VRAM — 47.1 versus 94.9 gigabytes — by replacing the 13.5 GB reference model copy with 42 MB LoRA adapters.

Loss-based Detection achieves the lowest harmful score at 2.39% — the best across all 9 methods.

These results are validated across 3 datasets, 4 architectures, poison ratios from 0.05 to 0.20, and 3 attack types.


[Slide 33 — Deployment Guidelines]

For practical deployment: when facing adversarial triggers that mimic benign loss distributions — indicated by KL divergence below 0.5 — we recommend falling back to uniform defense. SelGrad includes a built-in diagnostic for this.

Architecture-specific guidelines: use default parameters for Llama-2 and Gemma families, enhanced multi-signal detection for Llama-3, threshold calibration for Qwen, and a quick KL divergence analysis on 100 samples for new architectures.


[Slide 34 — Future Work]

Looking ahead, we identify four research directions:

First, multi-signal detection — integrating activation clustering and attention anomalies, which directly addresses the Llama-3 limitation.

Second, adaptive defense parameters — using meta-learning to optimize weight and alignment parameters from task characteristics.

Third, adversarial-aware detection — defending against loss-mimicking attacks, which is the fundamental limitation of loss-based detection.

Fourth, cross-architecture scaling — extending to models above 30B parameters, full fine-tuning beyond LoRA, and quantized models.
